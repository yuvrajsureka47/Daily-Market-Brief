US and Iran Formally Sign MOU — Strait of Hormuz Reopening in Play
Washington and Tehran digitally signed their interim peace memorandum of understanding on June 18, with the formal ceremony set for today in Switzerland. A US official confirmed the MOU has taken legal effect, although Iran's timeline for fully reopening the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of globally traded oil flows — remains uncertain. Markets treated the signing as disinflationary: Brent fell toward $78, energy stocks sold off, and cyclical and tech equities rallied hard on Friday. This is the most significant geopolitical macro catalyst of 2026 so far. If Iran follows through on Hormuz reopening, a sustained oil correction would structurally ease inflation data from July onward, compressing the Fed's case for a rate hike.
Fed Holds Rates — But Warsh's First FOMC Lands Hawkish Shock
Kevin Warsh chaired his inaugural FOMC meeting on June 16 to 17, keeping the federal funds rate unchanged at its current level as widely expected. What surprised markets was the dot plot (a chart showing where Fed officials expect rates to go): 9 of 18 policymakers now project at least one rate hike in 2026, the sharpest hawkish turn since the Iran war began driving energy inflation above 23% year-on-year. Warsh also stripped forward guidance from the post-meeting statement, cutting it to just 130 words, signaling a new communication philosophy that gives markets less visibility into future moves. Markets now fully price a hike by October.

China
China's 15th Five-Year Plan Targets 4.5–5.0% Growth — Supply-Side Over Stimulus
Beijing's 2026 GDP target is set at 4.5 to 5.0%, down from prior years, reflecting a deliberate pivot toward quality growth over quantity. The 15th Five-Year Plan (2026 to 2030) prioritises technological self-reliance — R&D spending to rise at least 7% annually — and decarbonisation. Policy deliberately avoids broad demand stimulus, instead using targeted fiscal measures: the deficit is held at 4% of GDP with special bonds for strategic industries. For global markets, this means China will not deliver a consumption boom to absorb global output, keeping external demand pressure on Europe and APAC exporters elevated.

SpaceX Acquires Cursor AI for $60 Billion — Largest VC Startup Buyout in History
Just days after its record $85.7 billion Nasdaq IPO (the biggest in stock market history), SpaceX formally agreed to acquire Anysphere — the startup behind AI coding assistant Cursor — in an all-stock deal valued at $60 billion. Cursor generates approximately $2.6 billion in annualised enterprise revenue from clients including Stripe, Adobe, and Nvidia. The deal is a direct response to the failure of SpaceXAI (the division formed when SpaceX absorbed xAI, Elon Musk's AI company, in February 2026) to build a competitive AI coding tool. SpaceX also reportedly plans to launch a code repository platform called Origin, competing directly with Microsoft's GitHub. The market implications are significant: this consolidates the AI developer tools sector under major incumbents, with Anthropic now controlling roughly 50% of the AI coding assistant market.
Platform Transition
Google Migrates Developers from Gemini CLI to Antigravity CLI — Agentic AI Infrastructure Consolidates
Google confirmed on June 18 that its Antigravity CLI has replaced the old Gemini CLI as part of a broader migration to the Antigravity ecosystem — Google's AI-assisted software development platform built around Gemini 3.5 Flash and the upcoming Gemini 3.5 Pro. This is more than a tooling update: it represents Google's strategic move to lock developers into an integrated agentic coding environment, directly competing with Cursor (now SpaceX), GitHub Copilot (Microsoft), and Claude Code (Anthropic). The AI coding platform wars are now a four-horse race between the largest technology companies in the world, with the prize being developer workflow infrastructure worth hundreds of billions in annual software productivity.
