United States
PCE Inflation Hits 4.1% YoY in May — Highest Since April 2023
The BEA released May PCE data on June 25, clocking headline PCE at 4.1% year-on-year and core PCE (ex food and energy) at 3.4% — both meaningfully above the Fed's 2% target. Monthly prints came in at +0.4% headline and +0.3% core. The energy shock from the US-Iran conflict is still transmitting through the price chain even as oil retreats. This keeps the Fed's hands tied: oil is falling, but underlying inflation is still sticky and accelerating. Markets are now pricing a 68% probability of a September rate hike, up from 29% just a week ago.
Fed Chair Warsh Keeps Rates at 3.50%–3.75% But Turns Hawkish
Kevin Warsh held the federal funds rate steady at his first FOMC meeting last week, but the dot plot — which maps out where officials expect rates to go — shifted from signaling a cut to signaling a hike by year-end. Nine of 18 FOMC members now expect at least one rate increase in 2026. The Fed also revised its core PCE forecast for 2026 from 2.7% to 3.3% and does not see inflation returning to 2% before 2028. Higher rates are a direct headwind for AI hyperscalers (large cloud tech companies) that have been debt-financing their infrastructure buildout — Barclays estimates $200 billion in new debt issuance this year by hyperscalers alone.
Apple Drops 6% on MacBook and iPad Price Hikes Driven by Memory Crisis
Apple announced sweeping overnight price increases on June 25 — MacBook Air up $200 to $1,299, base MacBook Pro up $300 to $1,999, iPad and Apple TV also raised 15% to 25% — citing an "unprecedented" global memory chip shortage caused by AI data center demand. The company explicitly said it could no longer absorb the costs. Apple shed about $265 billion in market cap in a single session. This is a direct consumer-level consequence of AI capital expenditure pulling memory supply away from electronics manufacturers. JPMorgan warned iPhone price hikes may follow in September when the iPhone 18 launches.

AI Watch
Earnings Signal
Micron Earns $41.5B in a Quarter — Revenue Quadrupled, Gross Margins Hit 85%
Micron Technology reported fiscal Q3 2026 revenue of $41.46 billion against expectations of $35.7 billion — a massive beat. Adjusted EPS came in at $25.11 versus the $20.78 consensus. Gross margins reached a record 84.9%. The company simultaneously announced 16 multi-year "take-or-pay" supply agreements locking in approximately $100 billion in minimum contracted revenue and $22 billion in upfront customer cash. Management guided for roughly $50 billion in Q4 revenue, more than quadruple the prior year. The results confirm that the AI memory trade is structural — not cyclical — and that supply discipline will sustain elevated pricing for at least 18 more months per management commentary. Memory has effectively become the new constraint in the AI buildout, displacing GPUs as the scarce resource.
Supply Chain Impact
"RAMageddon" Hits Consumer Electronics — AI Data Centers Starve Device Makers of Memory
Memory chip makers Micron, Samsung, and SK Hynix have redirected production toward high-bandwidth memory (HBM) used in AI accelerators like Nvidia's GPUs, creating a severe shortage of conventional DRAM and NAND flash used in laptops, tablets, and game consoles. The consequence: Apple, Microsoft (Xbox up $100–$150), and Valve (Steam Machine priced at $1,919) have all raised hardware prices sharply. JPMorgan estimates that DRAM and NAND could rise from 10–15% to more than 45% of an iPhone's component cost by 2027. This is the first clear consumer-facing inflation signal from the AI infrastructure buildout — AI capex is now competing directly with consumer electronics supply chains, and the loser, for now, is the consumer.
Infrastructure Deal
Qualcomm Nearly Doubles Non-Handset Revenue Target to $40B by 2029; SK Hynix Eyes $29B Nasdaq Listing
Qualcomm rose 9% on June 25 after almost doubling its 2029 non-smartphone revenue forecast to $40 billion (up from $22 billion), announcing new data center partnerships with Microsoft and Meta, and reiterating a partnership with AI companies to embed Qualcomm compute into AI inference hardware. This signals that Qualcomm's strategic pivot from mobile chips to AI infrastructure is accelerating. Separately, SK Hynix — maker of the HBM chips inside Nvidia's AI accelerators — is targeting a $29 billion US Nasdaq listing as soon as July 10. A successful listing would be one of the largest on US markets and would further entrench the memory-as-AI-infrastructure investment thesis.

This rotation is likely to persist as long as memory supply remains constrained.
