Key Takeaways
1
The Strait of Hormuz is the single most important variable in global markets right now — not the Fed, not earnings, not AI. Oil price controls inflation expectations, which control the 10Y yield, which controls the cost of capital for every risk asset on the planet. Watch tanker traffic through the Strait, not peace deal press conferences. Confirmed reopening is the event, not the announcement.
2
India's market is currently being held up by domestic institutional conviction, not foreign flows. FIIs have sold aggressively in April and May — cumulative outflows have already surpassed all of last calendar year. DIIs absorbing this is impressive but has a limit. If oil stays elevated and the current account deficit widens further, the RBI will face a currency defense vs. growth tradeoff that the market is not pricing.
3
The SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic IPO pipeline is a macro event, not just a tech story. These three listings together could absorb several hundred billion dollars of institutional reallocation. This is the largest single wave of new public equity supply since the dot-com era. Existing tech positions — particularly in index-weight names like Nvidia, Microsoft, and Alphabet — will face mechanical selling pressure as portfolio managers make room. This rotation will likely begin before the listings, not after.
4
Bitcoin failing to close above its 200-day MA at $82,228 for five consecutive sessions — despite both regulatory tailwind (CLARITY Act) and geopolitical optimism — tells you the macro regime is still hostile to crypto. The 4.56% 10Y yield makes risk-free capital genuinely competitive. Bitcoin's unlocking event is a confirmed Fed pivot signal or a material drop in oil that removes the rate-hike probability from the table. Until then, expect $70K–$82K consolidation.
5
This week's critical data: China May NBS PMI (a sub-50 print would be globally bearish), US Q1 GDP revision and initial jobless claims on May 28, and any confirmed Strait of Hormuz shipping data. Europe is already in PMI contraction territory. If China joins, two of the world's three largest economic blocs will be contracting simultaneously — and the Fed will be unable to cut because oil is keeping US inflation elevated. That is a genuine stagflation trap with no historical clean exit playbook.
